Soon You could Become a Superhero

Movies featuring heroes with superpowers, such as flight, X-ray vision or extraordinary strength, are all the rage. But while these popular characters are mere flights of fancy, scientists have used nanoparticles to confer a real superpower on ordinary mice: the ability to see near-infrared light. Today, scientists report progress in making versions of these nanoparticles that could someday give built-in night vision to humans.

On the road to human night vision

Earlier in the year, researchers successfully gave mice night vision using UCNPs enzymes. What could pose an issue for use in humans: the UNCPs were made using rare earth elements erbium and ytterbium. Although they remained in the mice’s eyes for 10 weeks without harming them, the team were concerned that they could cause side-effects in humans. “The UCNPs are inorganic, and there are some drawbacks there” said one of the researchers. “The biocompatibility is not clear, we need to improve the brightness of the nanoparticles for human use.” Their solution was to redevelop the night vision material using organic dyes, which would be safer. Today’s presentation demonstrate successful use of this dye in mice.

The Amazing Design of the Human Eye

Human eyes take some time to adjust when someone in the room suddenly turns off the light. There are times when very bright lights at one’s workplace sometimes cause fatigue and discomfort. Pupils have a property to expand and contract according to the change in intensity of light. This affects the human brain significantly which is why the right intensity of light at the right place is very important.

How Cats See in the Dark

A cat's eye is built to collect light. The rounded shape of the cornea helps capture and focus light, eye placement on the face allows for a 200° field of view, and cats don't have to blink to lubricate their eyes. However, the two factors giving cats the advantage at night are the tapetum lucidum and composition of light receptors on the retina. Retinal receptors come in two flavors: rods and cones. Rods respond to changes in light levels (black and white), while cones react to color. About 80 percent of the light receptor cells on a human retina are rods. In contrast, around 96 percent of the light receptors in a cat's eyes are rods. Rods refresh more quickly than cones, too, giving a cat faster vision.
